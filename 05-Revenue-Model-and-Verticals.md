## 6.1 Revenue Stack Overview

Based on comparable-venue revenue composition (Texas Renaissance Festival-scale operator financial breakdowns), a mature multi-revenue-stream event business typically splits roughly as:

| Stream | Typical share of gross revenue (mature Renn-faire-scale comparable) |
|---|---|
| Gate/ticket admission | 50–60% |
| Vendor booth rentals/revenue share | 15–25% |
| F&B, merchandise, and other add-ons | remainder |

**This ratio is a mature-stage reference point, not a Year 1 target** — at our scale, ticket revenue will likely represent an even larger share of the total in Year 1 (vendor and merchandise ecosystems take time to build, per `01-Market-and-Comparables.md`'s honest market-sizing discussion). It's useful primarily as a **directional sanity check**: if vendor/merchandise revenue is expected to exceed ~25% of the total in a financial model, that assumption should be treated as optimistic and stress-tested.

## 6.3 Food & Beverage (Recap + Margin Note)

Per `04-Operations-and-Event-Design.md` §5.5: drop-off/buffet catering runs **$18–$35/person** (cost basis). If the Company operates F&B directly (rather than just passing through a caterer's price), **standard food-service markup practice supports pricing the guest-facing meal-plan add-on at roughly 1.5–2x the raw catering cost basis** to cover staffing, spoilage, and a real margin — i.e., a $25/person catering cost supports a **$35–$50/person guest-facing meal plan price**, consistent with how optional add-on meal plans are typically positioned at comparable outdoor events. **Alcohol remains a deferred, later-phase line** per `03-Regulatory-Legal-and-Insurance.md` §4.2.

## 6.4 Vendor Stalls

Real, sourced revenue-share/rental data from Renaissance-faire-scale comparables:

- **Booth rental fees** are reported to contribute **15–25% of gross revenue** at a mature Renn-faire-scale operation — this is a real, meaningful revenue category once the vendor ecosystem is built, not a minor add-on.
- **Two structural models exist industry-wide:**
  1. **Flat booth rental fee** — vendor pays a fixed fee regardless of their sales performance. Lower risk for the Company (guaranteed revenue), lower risk for high-performing vendors (no revenue share), but **higher risk for the Company if vendor turnout/quality is inconsistent** in early years.
  2. **Revenue-share model** — vendor pays a percentage of their sales (a cited example uses **~20%**) instead of/in addition to a flat fee. This **aligns incentives** (the Company is motivated to drive foot traffic to all vendor stalls) and is lower-risk for vendors uncertain about a new, unproven event — which is a real consideration for us in Year 1, when we have no track record to point new vendors to.
- **Recommendation for Year 1:** a **low flat fee plus a modest revenue-share component** (a hybrid, which is a known structure in the events industry) reduces vendor risk enough to attract vendors to an unproven first-year event, while still giving the Company some guaranteed revenue floor per stall. Exact fee/percentage should be benchmarked against comparable regional faire/market events once specific vendor categories (armor, food, crafts) are identified — **no site-specific vendor fee schedule exists yet**; this is a Year 2+ negotiation, not a Year 1 launch requirement, since Year 1 vendor participation will likely be small/invitational given the 30–50 person first-event scale.

## 6.5 Merchandise

Real margin data from the branded-merchandise/screen-printing industry:

| Production method | Gross margin | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Screen printing (bulk, 24+ units) | **50–70%** | Best margin at event scale; requires upfront order commitment and $20–$200 setup cost |
| Print-on-demand (no inventory risk) | 20–40% | Better for testing designs before committing to bulk orders |
| DTF (direct-to-film) transfer | 35–55% | Middle ground — lower setup cost than screen printing, better margin than POD |

- **Typical event/fan merchandise markup is 100–200% over production cost** — e.g., a $7–$12 production-cost shirt commonly retails at $25–$35 at live events, which is a well-established, guest-accepted price point (not something we'd be inventing).
- **Recommendation for Year 1:** start with **print-on-demand or small-batch DTF** for initial designs (avoids tying up capital in unsold inventory before the brand/community is established), and **shift to bulk screen printing once a design is proven to sell** at repeat events — this sequencing avoids the single biggest merchandise-margin killer identified in the sourced data: **committing to a bulk screen-printing order (24+ units) for an unproven design**, which can turn a 50–70%-margin opportunity into a net loss on unsold stock.
- Merchandise is a genuinely **high-margin but currently small-scale** revenue line for Year 1 — realistic to treat as a modest supplementary revenue stream, not a primary pillar, until attendance and brand recognition grow.

## 6.7 Third-Party Venue Rental / Corporate & Private Bookings

This is the vertical most directly supported by real precedent: **Cooper's Lake Campground operates as a specialized event venue used by *multiple different organizations*** (Pennsic War is its largest and best-known event, but the venue model itself — a landowner renting a purpose-built, multi-terrain site to outside event producers — is the structural precedent for treating our own land as a **rentable asset independent of our own branded events**, once it exists.

**Why this matters for capital planning:** a piece of land purpose-built for immersive outdoor events (varied terrain, camping infrastructure, sanitation, parking) has **rental value to other LARP groups, reenactment groups, corporate retreat organizers, and private event bookers** (per real farm/barn-venue rental data since sourced in `09-Pilot-Test-Event-Plan.md` §9.3, which shows **$2,000–$10,000/weekend** for comparable-scale farm/event venues, 120–450 capacity) — **independent of whether our own branded events are running that weekend.** This directly addresses the capital-intensity concern flagged in `02-Site-and-Real-Estate.md`: the land doesn't have to sit idle between our own quarterly events; it can generate revenue on the off-weekends once basic infrastructure (parking, sanitation, water) exists.

**Recommendation:** treat this as a **Year 2+ vertical**, once the site has enough basic infrastructure to be rentable to a third party without the founders' own operational involvement (i.e., a group renting the *land*, not needing the Company's own staff/NPCs/plot). This is a genuinely underexploited revenue lever relative to how much attention it's likely to get in typical planning — flagging it explicitly here so it isn't lost in `08-Financial-Projections.md`.

## 6.8 Revenue Prioritization for Year 1–2

Given the founders' capital constraints and the honest data above, the realistic Year 1 revenue priority order is:

1. **Ticket/lodging revenue** (the only proven, immediately available revenue stream at Year 1 scale)
2. **Optional F&B add-on** (straightforward to operate, modest margin, low capital requirement)
3. **Photo/video package sales** (genuine differentiator, low marginal cost once staff are already budgeted for the guest-service role)
4. **Small-scale/invitational vendor stalls** (real revenue category, but meaningfully scales only once the event has a track record — treat Year 1 vendor revenue as a bonus, not a planning assumption)
5. **Print-on-demand merchandise** (small, high-margin-percentage but low-absolute-dollar category at Year 1 attendance levels)
6. **Third-party venue rental** (Year 2+ vertical, contingent on basic infrastructure being in place — the most important *long-term* de-risking lever for the land-idle-time problem, but not a Year 1 revenue line)

---

## 6.9 Key Takeaways Feeding Later Sections

1. **Ticket revenue will carry Year 1–2 disproportionately** — the 50–60% gate/25% vendor mature-market split is a later-stage benchmark, not a Year 1 assumption; `08-Financial-Projections.md` should not assume meaningful vendor/merchandise revenue in Year 1.
2. **Vendor stalls should use a hybrid low-flat-fee-plus-revenue-share model in early years** to reduce vendor risk on an unproven event, shifting toward flat fees as the event builds a track record.
3. **Merchandise should start print-on-demand/small-batch, not bulk screen printing** — avoids the real risk of unsold bulk inventory on unproven designs, even though bulk screen printing has the better margin ceiling (50–70%) once a design is proven.
4. **Third-party venue rental (the Cooper's Lake/Pennsic model) is a genuine, underused Year 2+ lever** for monetizing the land between the Company's own quarterly events — this should get explicit attention in `07-Startup-Costs-and-Capital-Plan.md` and `08-Financial-Projections.md`, not be an afterthought.
5. **No F&B, merchandise, or photo/video price points are finalized** — the ranges here are grounded in real industry data but require the founders' operational decisions (in-house vs. contracted staff, product scope) before becoming firm numbers in the financial model.
