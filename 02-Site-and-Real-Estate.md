## 3.1 Acreage Sizing — Year 1 vs. Maturity

Industry design benchmarks (RoverPass campground planning guide, corroborated by RV/campground zoning codes):

| Site type | Space per unit | Density/acre | Minimum viable acreage |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tent-only (BYO camping) | 1,000–1,500 sq ft | 10–15 sites/acre (tight, budget spacing) | 2–3 acres bare minimum, 5+ recommended |
| Glamping/furnished tent or cabin | 2,000–5,000 sq ft | ~4–8 units/acre | 10–15 acres for a "premium" feel |
| Mixed-use (BYO + glamping + RV + activity space) | Varies | — | 10–20 acres |

**Applying this to our stated targets:**

- **First event (30–50 people):** even at loose, immersive (non-budget-RV-park) spacing, this fits comfortably on **5–10 developed acres** — but that figure covers *camping only*. Add: a central "set" area for the theme (market square, quest stations), a communal fire pit area, parking (see below), a staging/NPC-prep area out of guest sightlines, and a buffer zone of natural terrain for immersion and neighbor privacy. Realistic total for a *usable* Year 1 site: **15–25 acres**, even though camping itself doesn't need that much — the extra acreage buys you terrain variety (woods for quests, open field for structures) and buffer from adjacent landowners, which matters for both immersion and noise complaints (see `03-Regulatory-Legal-and-Insurance.md`, pending).
- **~200 people/event (end of Year 1 target):** scaling BYO camping alone to 200 guests at loose spacing (~5–6 sites/acre for privacy, assuming ~3 people/site average) needs roughly **7–10 acres just for tents**, plus proportionally more parking, staging, and set space. **30–50 total acres** is a defensible planning number at this scale, still well short of a "buy everything up front" ask.
- **Full maturity (multiple standing themed zones — Halfling Hollow + Colonial Village + Wastelands simultaneously, each with some permanent/semi-permanent structures):** this is structurally closer to what **Knight Realms (200 acres)** and **Texas Renaissance Festival (255 acres total: 55 developed + 200 camping)** operate on. **100–200+ acres** is the realistic long-run ceiling if the plan is to eventually run multiple standing themed sets rather than constantly re-skinning one shared field.

**Recommendation:** target an initial purchase in the **30–60 acre range** — enough to comfortably run the Year 1 event cadence (30–50 people scaling to ~200) with room for a second and third themed zone, while keeping land cost (see `01-Market-and-Comparables.md` §2.2) inside a crowdfunding-realistic budget. Treat "buy 100+ acres on day one" as a **later-phase acquisition or adjacent-parcel expansion**, not a startup requirement — this directly reduces the initial capital ask, which matters given the stated preference for crowdfunding over personal capital or heavy debt.

## 3.2 Site Selection Criteria

Ranked by how much each factor is validated by our comparables/data vs. how much is planning judgment:

1. **Mixed terrain (open field + woodland + water feature).** Every comparable venue we found (Cooper's Lake: lake + woods + mowed fields; Knight Realms; TRF) explicitly advertises a **mix of terrains** as a selling point — flat cleared land alone is not sufficient for immersive multi-theme LARP (Wastelands needs different visual terrain than Halfling Hollow). A water feature (pond/creek) adds production value but **triggers additional regulatory scrutiny** (floodplain/wetlands rules — building or grading near protected wetlands requires Army Corps of Engineers approval per RoverPass's campground FAQ). Net: **desirable but not required**, and a genuine cost/complexity trade-off, not a free win.
2. **Road access and capacity.** TRF's own public complaints about single 2-lane road congestion at 60,000+ single-day attendance is a direct cautionary data point. At our Year 1 scale (30–200 people) this is a minor concern, but site selection should still avoid single-lane, poor-condition, or seasonal (unpaved/flood-prone) access roads, since **county emergency services (fire/EMS) access time** is also a permitting and insurance underwriting factor.
3. **Distance from nearest population center / neighbor buffer.** Cited barn-venue permitting data: **neighbor objection is reported as the number one reason Conditional Use Permits get denied** for rural event venues. A site with sufficient natural buffer (tree line, distance, topography) between the event area and the nearest non-participating residence reduces noise-complaint and permit-denial risk substantially. This is a **judgment-based sourcing criterion**, not something we have a specific footage rule for — recommend a minimum of several hundred feet of buffer/tree cover to the nearest residential neighbor as a starting due-diligence checklist item, to be confirmed against the specific county's noise ordinance once a site is shortlisted.
4. **Soil suitable for septic (percolation test required before purchase, not after).** See §3.3 — this is a hard technical gate, not a preference. A parcel that fails a perc test may be unusable for anything beyond porta-potty-only events.
5. **Existing power/well or feasible off-grid plan.** Off-grid generator power is standard at LARP/reenactment events (Pennsic, most regional LARP campgrounds run this way) — **grid power is a "nice to have," not a requirement** for Year 1. A well is more important since potable water for staff/medical is generally expected.
6. **Zoning classification and county permitting posture.** Kentucky and Tennessee administer special-event/mass-gathering permitting **at the city/county level**, not statewide (confirmed in `01-Market-and-Comparables.md` §2.3). This means the *same acreage and terrain* can be dramatically easier or harder to permit depending on which specific county it's in — **this must be checked before, not after, a purchase offer**, ideally by a pre-purchase call to the county planning/zoning office asking specifically about "outdoor recreational assembly use" or "campground/special event venue" zoning classification and conditional-use-permit process.

## 3.3 Utilities, Septic, and Sanitation — Real Cost Data

This is one of the more concrete, quotable cost categories available to us:

| Item | Cost range | Source/notes |
|---|---|---|
| Well drilling | $3,000–$15,300 (avg ~$5,500) | Depends on depth (100–400 ft typical), $25–$65/ft |
| Residential-scale septic (small event/staff use only) | $3,500–$12,500 | Conventional system; aerobic/mound systems run $10,000–$20,000+ on difficult soil |
| **Commercial-scale septic** (needed once event size exceeds residential capacity) | **$15,000–$40,000** (small commercial) up to **$30,000–$80,000** (mid-scale, restaurant-equivalent flow) | Per commercial septic engineering data; **a cited industry rule of thumb is that events of 150+ guests typically exceed residential septic capacity** |
| Campground-style per-site wastewater design flow (no hookups) | 75–100 gallons/day per site | Standard commercial design guideline used by septic engineers sizing campground systems |
| Portable sanitation (porta-potties) as an interim/Year-1 solution | Not itemized here — needs local vendor quote | Standard practice at outdoor LARP/reenactment events (Pennsic, most regional LARPs) instead of built septic in early phases |

**Practical sequencing implication:** a **full commercial septic system is not a Year 1 requirement**. Given our 30–50 person first-event target is well under the 150-guest threshold cited above, **portable sanitation is both industry-standard and financially rational for Year 1**, deferring the $15,000–$80,000 commercial septic capex until attendance and event frequency justify it (likely around the point we're approaching the 200-person/event target). This should be reflected as a **phased capex item** in `07-Startup-Costs-and-Capital-Plan.md`, not a Day 1 cost.

Well + septic combined for a modest staff/small-permanent-structure setup: budget **$15,000–$30,000** at Year 1 scale (well + residential-grade septic for staff/admin facilities), with a **separate, later capital tranche of $15,000–$80,000** for commercial-grade sanitation once attendance regularly exceeds ~150 guests/event.

## 3.4 Buy vs. Lease vs. Lease-to-Own

The user's original concept explicitly calls for a **parent company/board that collectively owns the land** — ownership is the stated goal, not just a default. Weighing the alternatives honestly against that goal and the stated capital constraints (low personal cash appetite, crowdfunding-first):

| Option | Pros | Cons | Fit |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Buy outright (cash or seller financing)** | Full control; can build permanent/semi-permanent structures (glamping platforms, colonial-village buildings) without landlord risk; real estate is bank-financeable collateral; matches stated governance model (board *owns* the land) | Requires the largest upfront capital; illiquid; carries property tax and long-term maintenance liability regardless of event revenue | **Matches the stated vision most directly** — the end-state goal |
| **Lease (multi-year)** | Lowest upfront capital; can "test" a location/market before committing | Landlords are frequently unwilling to let a tenant install $15k–$40k glamping platforms or dig commercial septic without very long lease terms; undermines the "board owns the land" model entirely; the RoverPass campground FAQ explicitly warns that **long-term infrastructure investment requires strong lease protections that many landlords won't offer** | Workable **only** as a short-term bridge, not the end state |
| **Lease-to-own / seller-financed purchase option** | Lets the venture start running events (generating revenue and crowdfunding proof-of-concept) on a smaller cash outlay while a purchase option or seller-financing note converts to ownership over time | Requires finding a motivated rural seller willing to structure this way — not guaranteed to exist for a specific target parcel; adds legal complexity (needs a real estate attorney to structure correctly) | **Worth actively pursuing given the stated low-cash-outlay/crowdfunding preference** — this is the most realistic way to reconcile "we want to own land" with "we don't want to put up much personal cash up front." |

**Recommendation:** pursue **lease-to-own or seller-financed purchase** as the primary target structure during land search, with **outright cash/bank-financed purchase** as the fallback once crowdfunding capital is actually in hand. Straight leasing should be treated as a **fallback-of-last-resort** only, since it structurally conflicts with the board-ownership model and limits the permanent-structure investment (glamping tents, standing colonial buildings) that differentiates this venture from a bare rented field.

## 3.5 County Shortlist Approach (Central KY / Northern TN)

We do not yet have parcel-level or county-specific zoning confirmation — that requires direct calls to county planning offices, which should happen once 2–4 candidate counties are chosen. Based on the land-cost data already gathered (`01-Market-and-Comparables.md` §2.2), the counties worth investigating first are ones that are:

- **Rural/non-metro** (to stay in the $1,600–$6,500/acre band rather than the $40,000+/acre band near Lexington, Louisville, or Nashville).
- Within a **reasonable drive time of a mid-size population center** (for attendee travel, staffing, and emergency services response — a county that is *too* remote creates its own EMS/access problems per §3.2).
- Illustrative candidates surfaced by the land-price data (flagged **TO VALIDATE — not zoning-checked**): eastern Kentucky counties, and Tennessee counties such as Hancock, Perry, or Wayne (cited in `01-Market-and-Comparables.md` as low-cost-per-acre) — these are **starting points for research, not recommendations**, since we have not verified zoning, road access, or terrain quality for any specific parcel in these counties.

**Concrete next action (not yet done, requires your direct involvement or a licensed KY/TN commercial real estate agent):** shortlist 3–5 candidate counties within your practical travel range, then call each county's planning/zoning office to ask specifically: (1) is "campground," "outdoor recreation," or "special event venue" a permitted or conditional use in their rural/agricultural zoning classifications, and (2) what their special-event/mass-gathering permit process looks like.

## 3.6 Timeline

Per RoverPass's campground development guidance (a directly relevant comparable, even though our first phase is lighter-weight than a full commercial campground):

- **Raw land to functional campground: 6–18 months total**, with **due diligence, zoning, and permitting typically the longest phase** (can run several months on its own, especially if a variance or conditional-use permit is required), followed by **3–9 months of physical construction/build-out**.
- Because our **Year 1 event does not require full commercial septic or permanent structures** (§3.3), our actual "land to first event" timeline can likely run toward the **shorter end of that range (6–10 months)** — clearing a field, running a perc test, securing a temporary/special-event permit, and renting porta-potties is materially faster than building a full campground.

## 3.7 Key Takeaways Feeding Later Sections

1. **Target 30–60 acres for the initial purchase** — enough for Year 1 through the 200-person milestone plus a second themed zone, without over-committing capital to land that outpaces near-term revenue.
2. **Portable sanitation defers $15,000–$80,000 of commercial septic capex** until attendance justifies it — a concrete, phased capital-planning win for `07-Startup-Costs-and-Capital-Plan.md`.
3. **Lease-to-own/seller-financing is the most honest path** to reconcile the stated board-ownership goal with the stated low-personal-capital/crowdfunding-first preference — flag this for the founders' legal/real-estate advisor conversation early.
4. **Neighbor buffer and county-level permitting posture are as important as price-per-acre** — a cheap parcel next to hostile neighbors or in a county hostile to "assembly use" zoning is a false economy.
5. **No specific parcel or county has been zoning-verified yet** — this is the most important open action item before any capital commitment.
