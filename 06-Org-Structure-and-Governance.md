# Section 7: Org Structure and Governance

*Applies standard startup governance/equity practices to the LLC structure locked in per `03-Regulatory-Legal-and-Insurance.md` §4.1. Vesting mechanics differ meaningfully between an LLC and a corporation — flagged explicitly below rather than glossed over.*

---

## 7.1 Manager-Managed vs. Member-Managed LLC

Two structural options exist under Kentucky/Tennessee LLC law, and the choice has real governance consequences:

| Structure | How it works | Fit for a 2–5 founder team |
|---|---|---|
| **Member-managed** | All members participate directly in day-to-day management decisions | Common for small LLCs, but **prone to internal conflict and slow decision-making** as founder count grows past 2 — every member has equal say by default unless the operating agreement narrows this |
| **Manager-managed** | Members elect one or more managers (who may or may not also be members) to run day-to-day operations; members retain voting rights over major/reserved decisions only | **Recommended structure for this Company** — it directly implements the "parent company/board" governance model the founders have already described wanting, by formally separating day-to-day operational authority (the manager(s)) from major-decision voting rights (the full member/"board" group) |

**Recommendation:** adopt a **manager-managed LLC**, with the operating agreement designating either a single managing member or a small managing committee (e.g., 2 of the 3–5 founders) for daily operations, while reserving major decisions (see §7.4) for a full-member vote. This is a standard, attorney-drafted provision — not something unusual to ask for.

## 7.2 Ownership Split — Methodology, Not a Fixed Number

**There is no universal "correct" equity split formula** — this is confirmed across every founder-equity resource reviewed. What matters is **documenting the rationale**, since undocumented or purely "friendship-based" equal splits are a well-known source of later founder disputes. Real factors used industry-wide to weight a split:

- **Who originated the core idea/concept**
- **Time commitment** — full-time founders vs. part-time/advisory founders should not receive equal splits by default
- **Capital contribution** — a founder putting in cash (e.g., toward land acquisition or initial capex) has a different risk profile than a founder contributing only labor/expertise
- **Irreplaceable expertise** — e.g., a founder with specific LARP-event-running experience (per the founders' stated asset) or a founder providing land-related expertise brings something the others structurally cannot replace quickly

**Equal splits are common in practice but are explicitly flagged by multiple sources as a source of later governance friction** — specifically because an equal-vote structure has no natural tie-breaker. **Recommendation:** if the founder group leans toward an equal or near-equal split (which is reasonable for a 3–5 person founding team with roughly comparable time/capital commitment), the operating agreement should still designate an explicit tie-breaking mechanism (e.g., a rotating chair, a designated managing member with a tie-breaking vote reserved for operational — not major — decisions) rather than leaving deadlock unresolved.

**Action item, unresolved:** the actual founder count (stated as 3–5) and each person's expected time/capital contribution are not yet defined — this section provides the methodology; the actual percentages are a founder decision to make together, ideally with a neutral facilitator or startup attorney present given the known dispute risk.

## 7.3 Vesting — Adapted for an LLC (Important Structural Difference from a Corporation)

**Standard startup practice (confirmed across every source reviewed): 4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff** — no equity vests for the first 12 months, then 25% vests at the cliff, with the remaining 75% vesting monthly over the next 36 months. This exists specifically to prevent **"dead equity"** — a founder who leaves after a few months keeping a full, permanent ownership stake, which both demoralizes remaining founders and makes future fundraising harder (investors will not fund a cap table with meaningful unvested/dead equity sitting with a departed founder).

**Important: this standard is built around corporate stock vesting mechanics (Section 83 of the tax code, 83(b) elections) — an LLC does not issue "stock."** For an LLC, the equivalent structure is typically implemented via:
- **Vesting membership interests (capital or profits interests) directly in the operating agreement**, with the company holding a contractual repurchase/forfeiture right over unvested interests if a founder departs early — functionally equivalent to corporate vesting, but documented differently.
- **A "profits interest" structure** (a tax concept specific to partnerships/LLCs) is commonly used to grant a going-forward equity stake with more favorable tax treatment than a capital interest, particularly relevant if any founder equity is granted for future services rather than for capital already contributed.
- **This has real tax complexity** (profits interest safe harbor rules, potential need for an 83(b)-equivalent election depending on structure) — **this is a genuine "hire a startup attorney/tax advisor" action item**, not something to finalize from general research alone. Flagging this explicitly rather than presenting invented specifics as settled fact.

**Recommendation:** adopt the same **4-year/1-year-cliff vesting philosophy** for founder membership interests as the corporate-world standard, implemented via the operating agreement's membership-interest-vesting and repurchase provisions, with the specific tax structure (capital vs. profits interest) confirmed by counsel once the founder group and their respective capital/sweat-equity contributions are finalized.

## 7.4 Decision Rights — What Requires a Full Vote vs. Manager Discretion

Directly implementing the manager-managed structure from §7.1, a typical operating agreement reserves the following for a **full-member ("board") vote**, while everything else defaults to the managing member(s):

**Reserved for full-member vote (typical "major decision" list):**
- Land acquisition, sale, or any mortgage/lien against Company real property
- Taking on debt above a set threshold, or issuing new membership interests (including to crowdfunding investors)
- Admitting a new member/founder
- Amending the operating agreement itself
- Any merger, dissolution, or sale of substantially all Company assets
- Annual budget approval

**Left to managing member(s)/manager discretion (day-to-day operations):**
- Event scheduling and theme selection within the approved annual plan
- Hiring/contracting event staff, vendors, and service providers within budget
- Day-to-day vendor/insurance/permitting administration
- Marketing and guest-facing decisions

**Voting threshold recommendation:** major decisions typically require a **supermajority (e.g., two-thirds or unanimous for the most consequential items like dissolution or new debt)** rather than simple majority, precisely to prevent a bare majority from forcing a decision (like taking on land debt) that a large minority strongly opposes — this is standard practice for small multi-founder entities and should be a specific point of discussion when the operating agreement is drafted.

## 7.6 Key Takeaways Feeding Later Sections

1. **Manager-managed LLC structure is recommended** — it directly implements the founders' stated "parent company/board" governance model using a standard, well-understood LLC provision.
2. **No universal equity-split formula exists** — the founders need an explicit, documented conversation (ideally attorney-facilitated) weighing time commitment, capital contribution, and irreplaceable expertise, rather than defaulting to an undocumented equal split.
3. **4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff is the standard governance mechanism to prevent "dead equity"** — but for an LLC this must be implemented via membership-interest vesting/repurchase provisions in the operating agreement, not corporate stock mechanics. **This requires actual legal/tax counsel to finalize** — flagged as a genuine action item, not a DIY task.
4. **A supermajority voting threshold for major decisions (land, debt, new members, dissolution) is recommended** over simple majority, to protect against a bare majority forcing high-stakes decisions over strong minority objection.
5. **Role allocation across founders is a draft starting point**, to be finalized once the actual founder group and skill sets are known.
