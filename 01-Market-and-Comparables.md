## 2.1 Direct and Structural Comparables

There is no single existing business that matches this venture's exact model (permanent multi-theme LARP venue, tiered lodging, in-house production **and** third-party rental). Instead, it sits at the intersection of four proven models. Each is examined below with real data.

### A. Commercial recurring LARP campaigns (closest operational model)

**Knight Realms (Travance)** — Sparta, NJ
- Owns its own **200-acre site** (Camp Sacajawea), which it also uses to co-host the Sparta NJ Renaissance Festival — i.e., **one piece of land, multiple event brands**, exactly the structural idea proposed here.
- Running continuously since **1997** (28 years) — evidence that the model is durable but built slowly.
- Pricing is low relative to cost of land ownership: **$50/weekend event** for a player character, **free** for NPCs (volunteer performers), $20 first-time-player discount, $10 per extra "build" (XP) point.
- Events run **monthly**, not just 3–4/year — this is a mature-stage cadence, not a startup cadence.
- **Implication for us:** land ownership lets a group run a low-ticket-price, high-frequency, community-retention-driven game. But $50/weekend tickets cannot cover land acquisition debt service on their own — Knight Realms' land was very likely acquired debt-free or via patient capital over decades, not financed for quick payback. We should not assume monthly-cadence, low-ticket pricing is viable for a *new, leveraged* venue in years 1–3.

### B. Commercial multi-region LARP franchises

**Dystopia Rising** (multiple regional chapters: NY, NJ "The Pike," WI, TX, "The Anvil," etc.)
- Standard weekend ticket: **$75–$100**, with add-ons (extra XP $15–$30, "opt-out" of required volunteer NPC shift $125–$250).
- Ticket model requires **paid attendees to also work 4–6 hour unpaid "NPC" shifts** as monsters/extras during the event they paid for — this is the standard mechanism the LARP industry uses to staff antagonist roles cheaply. Directly relevant to our "in-character vendors/NPCs" staffing plan: budget for **mostly volunteer/trade-for-ticket NPC labor**, not a fully paid cast, is the market norm.
- Multiple regional chapters imply each chapter typically **rents a campground/retreat site** rather than owning land — ownership of a dedicated site (our model) is the exception, not the rule, in this industry. That is a real point of differentiation, but also removes the industry's usual technique for keeping fixed costs low (they don't carry land debt).

### C. Renaissance faires (closest revenue/scale ceiling comparable)

**Texas Renaissance Festival (TRF)**, Todd Mission, TX
- Site: **55 acres developed + ~200 acres of camping** — total footprint is a strong proxy for what "mature, high-attendance" land requirements look like.
- Attendance: **~475,000–680,000 per season**, run over **8 weekends** (source: Wikipedia, Houston Public Media, CultureMap Houston reporting on 2025 season: 492,910 patrons).
- Employs **~4,000 seasonal staff/performers**; hosts **350–500 independent vendor/merchant stalls**, which alone generate **over $10M/year** in vendor revenue (per TRF's own disclosed figures, reported by Houston Public Media).
- Founded **1974** — 51 years to reach this scale. TRF itself started on **15 acres** with **33,000** attendees in year one.
- **Implication for us:** TRF is the ceiling of this market category, not a realistic 5-year target. Its year-one attendance (33,000 over presumably several weekends) still dwarfs our stated Year 1 target (30–50 people/event, 3–4 events). That gap is fine — TRF proves the *category* can scale to a real regional business, but reaching that scale took five decades and it is a single-theme (Renaissance) faire, which simplified vendor and repeat-attendee marketing compared to our multi-theme concept.

### D. Owner/Operator vs. Event-Producer separation — direct precedent for our exact structure

**Pennsic War** (SCA) at **Cooper's Lake Campground**, Slippery Rock, PA
- **Three distinct legal entities** are involved: (1) Cooper's Lake Campground — the **private landowner/venue business**, (2) Pennsic War — the **event organizer**, (3) the SCA — the **membership organization**. This is direct real-world precedent for the parent-company/venue-vs-event-producer split described in the user's original concept.
- Scale: **10,000–11,384 attendees/year**, 17 days, held annually since the 1980s.
- Registration fee: **$225–$265** per adult for the full event (2025/2026 pricing), $40 for minors, $20 for infants — camping/land access is bundled into that fee; Cooper's Lake as landowner is paid by the event organizer for use of the land (exact commercial terms between Pennsic War and Cooper's Lake are not public).
- **Documented regional economic impact: $1.8 million into the Butler County, PA economy per year** (cited by Wikipedia's Pennsic War article, sourced to local reporting) — useful, publicly citable talking point for local/county government and investor conversations about regional economic benefit.
- **Implication for us:** this proves a landowner can profitably lease/host a large recurring event without being the creative producer. If we cannot staff full event production ourselves early on, **renting the venue to established outside LARP or reenactment groups is a validated, lower-risk revenue stream** while our own branded events (Halfling Hollow, Colonial Village, Wastelands) scale up in parallel.

### E. Glamping / tiered-lodging economics (for our "fully furnished themed tent" tier)

Per industry benchmark data (USDA glamping feasibility study aggregation):

| Tier | Capital cost per unit | Nightly rate | Annual revenue per site (seasonal) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Traditional tent campsite (BYO) | Minimal (grading/pad only) | $25–$50 | $2,500–$6,000 |
| Basic glamping (canvas tent, shared bathhouse) | $15,000–$40,000 | $100–$200 | $12,000–$25,000 |
| Premium/destination glamping | Higher, not itemized in source | $300–$600+ | $40,000–$90,000+ |

- **Implication for us:** the "fully supplied, themed canvas tent" tier maps directly onto the "basic glamping" row above — **budget roughly $15,000–$40,000 in capital per themed unit**, and price nightly access accordingly. This is a meaningful capex line item per unit that must be multiplied by however many premium units we plan for Year 1 capacity (e.g., 30–50 person first event does **not** require 30–50 premium units — most attendees at comparable events bring their own gear; premium units are a smaller, higher-margin subset).

### F. Franchise campground development cost (scale/cost ceiling reference only — NOT a fit for our model)

**KOA (Kampgrounds of America)** new-construction franchise:
- Minimum build requirement: **75 RV sites + 90 total sites**.
- Disclosed average new-construction cost: **$3.9M–$6.8M** (2024 FDD: $3.8M–$8.9M range including franchise fee).
- **Implication for us:** this figure is *not* directly comparable — KOA requires RV electrical/water hookups and a much higher minimum site count than a LARP venue needs. We cite it only to show that "build a full-service campground from scratch" is a multi-million-dollar undertaking at that specific scale; our venue does **not** need RV infrastructure and can be developed in far smaller, phased capital tranches (detailed in `07-Startup-Costs-and-Capital-Plan.md`).

## 2.2 Land Cost Reference — Central Kentucky / Northern Tennessee

Sourced from University of Kentucky Agricultural Economics (2025 farmland survey) and AcreValue/PrimeLandBuyers Tennessee county data:

| Region | Land type | Price/acre (2025) |
|---|---|---|
| Central Kentucky (Bluegrass/North Central subregion) | Cropland | $8,200–$8,700 |
| Central Kentucky (Bluegrass/North Central subregion) | Pasture | $5,600–$6,100 |
| Central Kentucky (Bluegrass/North Central subregion) | Woodland | $4,100–$5,200 |
| Eastern Kentucky | Pasture/Woodland | $1,600–$2,400 |
| Tennessee, statewide average (all land, Q2'23–Q1'26) | Mixed | ~$5,352 average |
| Tennessee rural counties (Hancock, Perry, Wayne) | Rural/small-acreage | $5,000–$19,000 |
| Tennessee, Nashville-adjacent counties (Williamson, Wilson, Davidson) | Developed/exurban | $41,000–$400,000+ (**avoid** — priced for residential development, not event land) |

**Practical read:** Woodland/pasture-classified rural acreage in **eastern Kentucky** or **non-metro Tennessee counties away from Nashville** is the realistic price band — roughly **$1,600–$6,500/acre** depending on county and land class, vs. $40,000+/acre near Nashville, Lexington, or Louisville metro rings. A 20–40 acre starter parcel (sized for a 30–50 person first event with room to grow toward ~200) would likely run **~$50,000–$260,000** in land cost alone before any development, at these per-acre rates — this is a rough order-of-magnitude estimate from the cited per-acre data, **not a quote**, and must be validated against actual listed parcels (see `02-Site-and-Real-Estate.md`, pending).

## 2.3 Regulatory Signal (Kentucky) — flag only, detailed in Section 3

Search of Kentucky municipal code confirms **special event / mass gathering permitting regimes exist at the city/county level** (e.g., Lexington, Paducah, Hazard have published special event permit processes; the KY Governor's office has issued mass-gathering orders in the past for public health purposes). This confirms the permitting pathway exists and is administered locally/county-by-county rather than under one single statewide "event venue" license — meaning **the specific county we select will materially affect the permitting process**, and this must be checked county-by-county once a target site is shortlisted, not assumed statewide.

## 2.4 Insurance Market Signal — flag only, detailed in Section 3

Confirmed: a real commercial insurance market exists for this exact niche.
- **K&K Insurance** — "Short-Term Event Insurance" for events under 12,000 attendees / up to 10 days; also underwrites vendor liability insurance for on-site vendors.
- **Anthony Insurance Services** — explicitly markets "**Affordable LARP Insurance**," describing the exact liability exposure we'd have (participant injury claims, property damage) and noting many venues **require** proof of liability coverage before permitting bookings.
- **Battle Merchant** — markets reenactment/LARP insurance for props and combat scenarios in this space as well (note: appears to be a German/EU-oriented insurer; relevance to a US venue needs confirming, flagged as **TO VALIDATE**).

**Implication:** insurance is obtainable, not a hypothetical blocker — but exact premiums require direct quotes from K&K or Anthony Insurance Services once our attendee counts, weapon/combat rules, and lodging structure (tents vs. built structures) are defined. We will not fabricate a premium number; Section 3 will list the quote-request checklist.

## 2.5 Market Sizing — Honest Data Gap

There is **no authoritative census of the U.S. LARP hobbyist population** — no government, trade association, or market-research figure exists that we can cite. Any number you see elsewhere online (e.g., "300,000 LARPers in the US") is typically an unsourced estimate repeated across blogs and should not be relied on for underwriting. What we *can* cite:

- **Pennsic** alone draws 10,000–11,384 people to a single event annually, drawing from a national/international pool of SCA members and affiliated reenactment groups.
- **TRF** draws 475,000–680,000 people per season, though the large majority of these are one-time general-public visitors, not LARP hobbyists — a fundamentally different, much larger addressable market (immersive entertainment/tourism) than committed LARPers.
- Multiple **regional Dystopia Rising chapters** (NY, NJ, WI, TX, and others) each independently sustain **recurring monthly-to-quarterly** attendance in the low hundreds per event — these are the closest scale analog to our stated ambitions.

**Conclusion for planning purposes:** our realistic near-term addressable market is the **existing regional community of LARP hobbyists, reenactors, and SCA-adjacent players within reasonable driving distance of central KY/northern TN** (a multi-state radius — likely KY, TN, OH, IN, WV, VA, NC, GA), plus a **secondary, much larger market of general-public "immersive experience" seekers** (bachelor/bachelorette parties, corporate team-building, photography/cosplay enthusiasts, "experience economy" consumers) who are not traditional LARPers but are drawn to novelty experiences — this second group is what Meow Wolf, escape rooms, and axe-throwing venues have successfully monetized, and is arguably a more scalable growth vector than pure LARP-hobbyist ticket sales for reaching the 200-person/event goal.

## 2.6 Key Takeaways Feeding Later Sections

1. **Ownership + rental hybrid (Pennsic/Cooper's Lake model) is the lowest-risk path**: hosting outside LARP groups/reenactors/corporate bookings can generate revenue on the land in weeks the Company isn't running its own branded event, smoothing the seasonality problem.
2. **Volunteer/trade-for-ticket NPC staffing is the industry-standard cost control** (Dystopia Rising model) — should be built into `04-Operations-and-Event-Design.md` rather than assuming paid actors for every role.
3. **Tiered lodging capex should track glamping industry unit economics** ($15–40k/unit for furnished canvas tents), not custom-invented numbers.
4. **Land should be sourced in rural, non-metro counties in eastern KY or non-Nashville-adjacent TN counties** for the $1,600–$6,500/acre band; anything near Lexington, Louisville, or Nashville will price out of range for a bootstrapped/crowdfunded startup budget.
5. **Insurance is available from named specialty carriers (K&K, Anthony Insurance Services)** — get real quotes early, since financing (bank or investor) will require a specific insurance cost line, not a placeholder.
6. **Reaching a 200-person/event Year 1 target is aggressive but not unprecedented** relative to Dystopia Rising chapter scale — however it will very likely require pulling from the larger "experience economy" general public market, not just existing LARP hobbyists, given the honest market-sizing gap above.
