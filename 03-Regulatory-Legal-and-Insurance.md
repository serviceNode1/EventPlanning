## 4.1 Business Entity Structure — LLC

**What an LLC gets you:**
- Pass-through taxation (no entity-level federal tax; profit/loss flows to members' personal returns).
- Liability shield for members against the Company's debts and against *other* members' misconduct (does not shield the Company itself from liability for injuries at events — that's what insurance is for, §4.5).
- Flexible governance — a multi-member LLC operating agreement can implement the "parent company/board" structure directly (member-managed with a managing board/committee, defined voting rights, capital-call provisions), which is well-suited to a 2–4 founder structure.

**Honest complication given the stated crowdfunding-first strategy:** the SEC's Regulation Crowdfunding rules (17 CFR §227.100) do **not** explicitly disqualify LLCs as issuers — an LLC is legally eligible to run a Reg CF raise. However, **in practice, LLCs issuing equity (membership units) to potentially dozens or hundreds of small Reg CF investors create a real administrative burden**: each investor becomes a legal LLC member entitled to a K-1 tax form every year, and multi-member LLC tax accounting scales in complexity with the member count in a way C-corp stock issuance does not. This is why most equity-crowdfunding-focused startups either (a) use debt-based or revenue-share crowdfunding instruments instead of membership units, or (b) convert to a C-corp before an equity raise. **This is not a reason to abandon the LLC decision** — it is a reason to be deliberate about *which* crowdfunding instrument is used (see `05-Revenue-Model-and-Verticals.md`/future capital-raise section): a **revenue-share note, SAFE-equivalent for LLCs, or debt crowdfunding instrument** avoids the K-1-per-investor problem while keeping the LLC structure intact. Flag this explicitly for your securities attorney when structuring the actual raise.

**Formation mechanics (not yet done):** Kentucky and Tennessee both allow standard domestic LLC formation (Secretary of State filing, operating agreement, EIN). Given the land will likely sit in whichever state we buy in, **forming the LLC in the same state as the land** is simplest (avoids foreign-LLC registration overhead) unless a specific reason (e.g., a Delaware LLC for investor familiarity) is raised by legal counsel later.

## 4.2 Permitting Landscape

Confirmed from direct research (not assumed):

- **Special event / mass gathering permits are administered at the city/county level in both Kentucky and Tennessee** — there is no single statewide "event venue license." (Confirmed via Lexington, Paducah, and Hazard KY municipal permit pages in `01-Market-and-Comparables.md` §2.3.)
- **Conditional Use Permits (CUPs)** are the typical mechanism for approving a rural parcel for "assembly use" (event venue) where the base zoning is agricultural/residential. Real cost/timeline data from barn-venue permitting (a directly comparable rural-event-venue case): **$300–$2,000 CUP application fee**, **2–4 months of county zoning committee hearings**, commonly resulting in **conditions** such as an events-per-year cap, a noise curfew (e.g., 10 PM), minimum parking ratios (~1 space per 3 guests), and proof of adequate sanitation capacity.
- **Neighbor notification and objection is part of the CUP hearing process** and is cited as the **single most common reason CUPs are denied** for rural event venues — reinforcing the site-selection buffer criteria in `02-Site-and-Real-Estate.md` §3.2.
- **Alcohol service, if offered**, requires a **separate liquor license** (roughly **$500–$8,000/year** depending on license type and jurisdiction, per comparable rural-venue data), plus — in many states — **potable well-water certification** if alcohol/food service is on the permit application, which can add **4–8 weeks of delay** if the well fails an initial water test. **Recommendation: budget for well-water testing at least 90 days before any alcohol/food permit application**, and treat alcohol service as an **optional, later-phase revenue line** (see `05-Revenue-Model-and-Verticals.md`, pending) rather than a Year 1 requirement, given this added regulatory friction.
- **Fire marshal review** is standard for any outdoor assembly event with open flame (our stated "large communal fire meeting area" is explicitly a fire-marshal-review item) and for any temporary structures (large tents rated for occupancy typically require a separate fire-code sign-off in most jurisdictions).

**Action item, unresolved:** none of this can be finalized into an exact cost/timeline until a specific county is shortlisted (per `02-Site-and-Real-Estate.md` §3.5) — the CUP fee, hearing cadence, and specific conditions (events/year cap, noise ordinance hours) vary by county and must be confirmed directly with that county's planning office.

## 4.3 Combat Safety Framework (Boffer/Foam Weapons) — DEFERRED TO PHASE 3+ (Not Active for Years 1–2)

Physical contact combat is the single largest driver of both our insurance cost and our legal exposure, so this needs a real operational framework, not just a policy statement.

**What the existing LARP industry actually does** (confirmed from published rulebooks of established boffer LARPs — Citadel LARP, Solitude LARP, Mythic Adventures, among others): there is **no single national certification body or government safety standard for boffer weapons** — safety is self-regulated via **each organization's own published weapon-construction rulebook**, typically specifying:
- Minimum foam core diameter and closed-cell foam padding thickness (commonly **at least ½ inch of closed-cell foam** on striking surfaces and pommels).
- Mandatory **reinforcement at weapon tips** to prevent the rigid core from breaking through the foam — cited as one of the most dangerous failure conditions in these rulebooks.
- A **mandatory weapons check/inspection station** at event check-in, where staff physically inspect every prop weapon against the written standard before it's allowed on-site.
- Ongoing spot-checks during the event for weapon degradation (foam wear, exposed cores).

**Implication for us:** before Year 1, the Company needs its **own written weapons-construction and combat-safety rulebook** (can be adapted from published standards like Citadel/Solitude/Mythic Adventures rather than invented from scratch — these are publicly available reference points, not something to copy verbatim without permission, but they establish the industry norm we should meet or exceed), plus:
- A **staffed weapons-check station** at every event (labor line item for `04-Operations-and-Event-Design.md`).
- **On-site first aid staffing** at every event — given physical contact combat, this is a near-mandatory operational cost, not optional. (Specific EMS/first-aid staffing requirements should be confirmed with the target county — some jurisdictions require a licensed EMT on-site above a certain attendance threshold for any "high-activity" permitted event; this must be checked once a county is chosen.)
- **Incident reporting and documentation procedures** — insurers underwriting this risk (§4.5) will expect to see a written incident log process as part of underwriting, not just after a claim.

## 4.4 Liability Waivers — What the Law Actually Says in KY and TN

**Applicability note given the non-combat phasing (§ top of document):** this section is **not** a combat-specific concern — it applies to **Year 1–2 operations as-is**. A non-combat, theater-of-mind event still carries real injury exposure: uneven camping terrain, trip/fall hazards, campfire burns, tent/structure collapse, food-service illness, heat/cold exposure, and general outdoor-recreation risk. **Every point below is written generally and applies regardless of combat status** — the waiver a Year 1 guest signs needs to hold up against these general recreational risks just as much as a future combat-inclusive waiver would need to hold up against blunt-force injury.

This is a place where the honesty-first instruction matters most: **a signed waiver is not a blanket shield**, and the two target states differ in important ways.

**Kentucky:**
- Kentucky courts require a waiver to **explicitly use the word "negligence"** or otherwise **clearly and unambiguously express intent to release the operator from liability for its own negligence** (*Hargis v. Baize*, Ky. 2005). A generic "I assume all risk" waiver without this specific language is **not reliably enforceable** in Kentucky.
- A waiver that meets this bar **has been upheld** in a directly relevant precedent: *Bowling v. Asylum Extreme, LLC* — a **paintball facility** case (a physical-impact recreational activity) where the release specifically listed eye injury as a contemplated risk, and was upheld because the injury that occurred was clearly within that contemplated risk. **Direct actionable lesson, applicable now: our Year 1–2 waiver should specifically enumerate the actual risks of a non-combat outdoor LARP event (trips/falls on uneven terrain, campfire burns, insect/wildlife exposure, food-service illness, structure/tent hazards), not use vague boilerplate** — the same "specifically enumerate the real risk" principle that made the paintball waiver enforceable applies directly to our current, non-combat risk profile.
- **Critical, non-negotiable finding, independent of combat status: Kentucky's Supreme Court (*Miller v. House of Boom Kentucky, LLC*, 2019) held that a parent CANNOT waive a minor child's right to sue a for-profit commercial recreational business.** This means **any waiver signed by a parent on behalf of a minor attendee is legally unenforceable in Kentucky**, full stop, regardless of how well it's drafted, **and regardless of whether the event involves combat** — a minor who trips on a tent stake or is burned at a campfire is just as unwaivable a claim as a combat injury would be. If the Company operates in Kentucky and allows minors to attend, the Company carries real, un-waivable liability exposure for any injury to a minor **today**, not just once combat is introduced. This remains a legitimate reason to consider an **18+ policy**, at least for the Kentucky location, or to carry meaningfully higher liability coverage limits and separate underwriting disclosure if minors are permitted.

**Tennessee:**
- Tennessee case law (*Henderson v. Quest Expeditions* — a whitewater rafting case, and similar precedent) generally **does enforce liability waivers for recreational/sporting activities**, on the reasoning that such activities are not "essential public services" and therefore don't implicate public policy protections the way, say, a hospital or utility waiver would. This reasoning applies just as directly to a non-combat outdoor camping/LARP event as it would to a combat one.
- However, **Tennessee will not enforce a waiver against gross negligence or willful/wanton conduct** (*Adams v. Roark*) — meaning if an injury results from the Company's reckless disregard for safety (e.g., known hazardous terrain left unmarked, a fire pit with no barrier, or spoiled food served without basic food-safety practice), **the waiver will not protect the Company** regardless of what it says. **Gross negligence is a general-safety-discipline standard, not a combat-specific one.**

**Bottom line for both states:** a waiver is a **necessary but not sufficient** control, **for Year 1–2 exactly as much as for a future combat phase**. The real risk-reduction tool is genuine operational safety discipline (marked hazards, fire-pit safety barriers, basic food-safety practice, clear campsite/terrain warnings, trained staff) — a state court is explicitly more likely to find "gross negligence" (which no waiver can excuse in either state) if that operational discipline is absent or informally enforced. **Waivers protect a company that is actually being careful; they do very little for a company that is not.**

## 4.6 Year 1–2 Non-Combat Risk and Insurance Profile (Operative Section for Current Planning)

Given the founders' decision to run a **non-combat, theater-of-mind experience for Years 1–2**, the risk and insurance profile is materially lighter than the combat scenario detailed in §4.3–4.5 above:

- **No weapons-check station or combat-safety rulebook is needed for Years 1–2** — the entire operational and staffing cost of §4.3 is deferred, not a Year 1 line item.
- **General liability insurance (K&K's Short-Term Event Insurance product, already confirmed to fit our attendee/duration scale) is very likely the primary policy needed**, rather than requiring a specialty combat/LARP liability rider from day one. This is a meaningfully simpler and likely cheaper underwriting conversation than the combat scenario — still needs a real quote, but the risk category being underwritten (outdoor camping/recreation event, no physical contact activity) is a well-established, commonly-insured category rather than a niche high-risk one.
- **Remaining real risk categories for Years 1–2** are the general recreational/outdoor hazards discussed in §4.4 (terrain, fire, food service, structures) — these still require genuine safety discipline and a properly drafted waiver, but they are **standard campground/outdoor-event risk**, not an elevated combat-injury risk category.
- **On-site first aid is still a reasonable, low-cost precaution** even without combat (basic outdoor-event first aid — heat exposure, minor injuries, allergic reactions) but is **not the near-mandatory, higher-tier medical staffing implied by physical combat** in §4.3.
- **The Kentucky minor-waiver finding (§4.4) is unaffected by the combat decision** — it remains a live consideration for Years 1–2 if minors are permitted to attend, independent of combat status.

**Net effect on near-term capital/operating plan:** deferring combat meaningfully **reduces both the insurance underwriting complexity and the required safety staffing** for Years 1–2 (`04-Operations-and-Event-Design.md` §5.2's "2+ dedicated security/safety staff" floor was justified specifically by combat-injury risk and should be revisited downward for the non-combat model — flagged for that document). This is a genuine, quantifiable de-risking of the Year 1–2 startup phase, not just a thematic change.

## 4.7 Key Takeaways Feeding Later Sections

1. **LLC is workable but the crowdfunding instrument needs care** — use a debt/revenue-share crowdfunding structure, not raw membership-unit sales to many small investors, to avoid a K-1-per-investor administrative burden. Flag for securities counsel.
2. **County-level CUP permitting** is the real gating process — realistic cost ($300–$2,000 fee) and timeline (2–4 months) now exist as planning numbers, but exact terms depend on the specific county chosen.
3. **Combat safety framework (§4.3) and combat/LARP insurance rider (§4.5) are deferred to Phase 3+** — not a Year 1–2 requirement, per the founders' non-combat phasing decision. The research is retained for when that phase is reached.
4. **Kentucky specifically cannot legally waive a minor's injury claim against a for-profit business, independent of combat status** — this is a real, binding legal fact (not a risk-averse suggestion) that applies to Years 1–2 as-is and should drive an explicit founder decision: 18+-only events, or accept uninsurable-by-waiver exposure for minors and price that risk into insurance/legal reserves.
5. **Waivers are necessary but insufficient in both states** — actual operational safety discipline (general outdoor/recreational hazards for now, combat safety later) is what prevents both injuries and "gross negligence" findings that void waiver protection entirely.
6. **The non-combat Years 1–2 model meaningfully simplifies the insurance conversation** — general liability (K&K) is likely the primary policy needed now, versus the more specialized/costly combat-LARP rider (Anthony Insurance Services) that becomes relevant only at Phase 3+. See §4.6.
7. **No real insurance premium quote exists yet** — this is a required action item before the financial projections in `08-Financial-Projections.md` can be finalized with real numbers rather than placeholders.
